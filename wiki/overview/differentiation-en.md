# Key Differentiation Points + Signature Scenes

Presents the 5 key differentiation points including genre gap capture, success/failure/comeback signature scenes, and streamer/viewer perspective analysis.

---

## 20-1. 5 Key Differentiation Points

1. **Genre gap capture**: Infantry-focused extraction is a red ocean. No extraction game exists with vehicles as the main element. We seize the position of 'vehicle extraction = this game'.

   > As of 2026, the infantry extraction market is saturated. However, not a single extraction game exists where vehicles serve as the main inventory and protagonist.

2. **Battlefield-style vehicle+infantry hybrid combat**: Not just tanks fighting tanks -- vehicles and infantry interweave in combat. Desert vehicle encounters during movement, dismounted infantry combat in towns, composite defense during Chinook holdout.

3. **Chinook holdout climax**: The moment you call the Chinook, your position is exposed across the entire map. The more cargo you've loaded, the more dangerous it gets. Every match races toward this climax.

4. **Free vehicle combination = strategy**: Truck formations mean large cargo + Chinook but are slow and become targets. No-truck formations are fast but limited to small cargo. The formation itself is the strategy.

5. **Dual-class convoy play**: The convoy splits across inside/outside/vehicle positions, experiencing the same event from different locations. Dual-class combinations actually change the outcome of a match.

## 20-2. Signature Scenes

### Success Scene 1 -- Cargo extraction after town operation
The convoy arrives at the outskirts of a military base and divides roles. Engineer+transport handle interior access clearance and loading; sniper+driver read bot movement patterns via drones from the perimeter; heavy weapons+gunner suppress the perimeter with vehicle-mounted guns. The sniper spots an aggressive bot approach first, the engineer finishes loading, and the convoy exits the town with the truck. **"We did it together."**

### Success Scene 2 -- Shaking pursuit and Chinook escape
Returning with loaded cargo, the escort vehicle spreads wide to scatter attention while the truck takes a detour behind a ridgeline. Heavy weapons cut off the pursuing vehicle to buy time; engineer+mechanic keep the damaged truck running. Beacon placed, 90-second holdout, all vehicles boarded, escape successful. **"It was dangerous, but we read it and survived."**

### Failure Scene 1 -- Truck lost during return
Truck-only formation with no escort vehicle. Predatory bots spot them first. Truck destroyed, 40-60% cargo dropped on-site, 5-10% returned via insurance. Some members escape on foot. **"We should have brought another escort vehicle. But at least it wasn't a total zero."**

### Failure Scene 2 -- Chinook holdout failure
20 seconds into the 90-second holdout, all 3 bot types converge. Without a medic, no revival is possible -- one person goes down, the line wavers. Truck destroyed 10 seconds before Chinook arrival. **"We shouldn't have called it here."**

### Comeback Scene -- Truck lost but partial recovery
Immediately after truck destruction, the convoy reassigns roles. Sniper reads the direction, heavy weapons buys time, medic stabilizes the line, engineer connects to the drop location. Infantry escape recovers some cargo/personal inventory. **"We thought it was over, but we saved what we could."**

## 20-3. Streamer/Viewer Perspective

What hooks viewers is not one person playing well, but scenes where roles are divided: the tension shift right after cargo loading, scenes where truck/escort/infantry are visible simultaneously, and moments where everything seems lost but gets turned around.

| Type | Target Emotion |
|------|---------------|
| Success scenes | We did it, we succeeded because we did it together, the risk was worth the reward |
| Failure scenes | Disappointing, but the reason makes sense, it wasn't completely futile |
| Comeback scenes | Thought it was over but there was still something left, one decision changed the entire match, this is why you press "one more game" |

## 20-5. Differences from Battlefield (Summary)

In Battlefield, once you get in a vehicle everyone drives off recklessly, and when combat ends you respawn and start over. In WR2, there is a systemic reason for vehicle convoys to stay together (truck protection/escort), losing combat means losing cargo, and when a match ends there is settlement and progression. We take Battlefield's "fun of fighting in vehicles" but not the "drive off recklessly and repeat respawn" pattern.

---

## 20-6. Differences from Tarkov (Summary)

Tarkov is a game where individual infantry collect gear and do full-stack customization. WR2 uses monolithic class swapping with no personal gear assembly. We don't take Tarkov's gear Tetris/unrealistically punishing design, but we do take the dealer NPC system and extraction/loss tension. WR2's core inventory is not a personal backpack but the vehicle cargo bed, and the progression axis is not a personal stash but truck trunk capacity.

---

## 20-7. Why This Game Is Needed

As of 2026, the infantry extraction market is saturated with Tarkov, Hunt: Showdown, Dark and Darker, etc. However, not a single extraction game exists where vehicles serve as the main inventory and protagonist. Combining this genre gap with Battlefield's vehicle+infantry hybrid combat texture enables an instantly distinguishable market position. The Chinook holdout climax is a structure that occurs every match, making it favorable for streamer content, and dual-class convoy play provides a depth of teamplay unseen in existing extraction games.
